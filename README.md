# 🥦 Food Deserts & Health Outcomes
### A Census Tract-Level Analysis of Geographic Food Access and Chronic Disease Rates

> **Harris School of Public Policy · Data & Policy (DAP) · Winter 2026 Final Project**

---

## Research Question

> *To what extent do "food deserts" correlate with specific health outcomes (diabetes, obesity, high blood pressure, and smoking rates) at the census tract level — and does this relationship hold after controlling for income, race, and urban/rural geography?*

Food deserts — areas where residents have limited access to affordable, nutritious food — are widely assumed to drive poor health outcomes. But is that relationship real, or is it really just a poverty effect? This project merges two national datasets at the census tract level to find out.

**Key finding:** Food desert tracts show a mean diabetes rate of **15.3%** vs. **12.3%** for non-desert tracts — a gap of **+3.1 percentage points** that remains statistically significant after controlling for income, race, vehicle access, and urban/rural status (p < 0.001).

## Data Sources

### Dataset 1 — USDA Food Access Research Atlas (2019)
| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Source** | USDA Economic Research Service |
| **URL** | https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-access-research-atlas/download-the-data |
| **Geography** | Census tract (2010 boundaries) |
| **Coverage** | ~72,000 U.S. census tracts |
| **Key variables** | `LILATracts_1And10` (food desert flag), `lapop1` (population >1 mile from grocery), `MedianFamilyIncome`, `TractSNAP`, `TractHUNV` |
| **Download** | Manual — Excel file (`FoodAccessResearchAtlasData2019.xlsx`) |

**What makes a "food desert"?** USDA defines a tract as a food desert (`LILATracts_1And10 = 1`) if it is both **low income** (poverty rate ≥20% OR median family income ≤80% of area median) **AND** **low access** (more than 1 mile from a supermarket in urban areas, or more than 10 miles in rural areas).

### Dataset 2 — CDC PLACES (2022)
| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Source** | Centers for Disease Control and Prevention |
| **URL** | https://chronicdata.cdc.gov/resource/cwsq-ngmh.csv |
| **Geography** | Census tract |
| **Coverage** | ~72,000 U.S. census tracts |
| **Key variables** | `DIABETES`, `OBESITY`, `BPHIGH`, `CSMOKING` (all model-based prevalence estimates, %) |
| **Download** | Programmatic via Socrata API (see `01_download_data.py`) |

### Merge
Both datasets are joined on the **11-digit census tract FIPS code** (`CensusTract` = `LocationID`). The inner join yields **56,327 tracts** across **48 states**.

## Key Findings

1. **Food desert tracts have significantly worse health outcomes across all four measures.** Diabetes: +3.1pp, Obesity: +6.6pp, High BP: +4.3pp, Smoking: +1.8pp (all p < 0.001).

2. **The food desert–diabetes gap persists after controlling for income.** OLS regression with controls for income, race, vehicle access, SNAP enrollment, and urban/rural status shows the LILA coefficient remains positive and significant, suggesting the food access effect is not purely a poverty effect.

3. **Income is the strongest single predictor** of health outcomes (Diabetes–Income r = −0.60), but food desert status adds independent explanatory power beyond income alone.

4. **Mississippi, Arkansas, and Alabama** have the highest food desert prevalence (>25% of tracts) and the highest mean diabetes rates nationally.

5. **Urban food deserts and rural food deserts differ substantially** in mechanism: urban deserts are driven by proximity, rural deserts by distance and vehicle access.

## Limitations

- **Cross-sectional data** — both datasets are snapshots (USDA 2019, CDC 2022), so we cannot establish causality.
- **Temporal mismatch** — USDA food access data is from 2019; health outcome data is from 2022. Some tracts may have changed food access status in between.
- **Ecological fallacy** — all analysis is at the tract level. Tract-level correlations do not necessarily hold for individuals within tracts.
- **USDA definition of food desert** is binary and may miss nuance. A tract 1.1 miles from a grocery store is counted the same as one 20 miles away.
- **No causal identification** — confounders not captured in this dataset (e.g., built environment, cultural food practices, healthcare access) may explain part of the observed relationship.
- **Missing data** — `lapop1` is missing for ~30% of tracts (rural tracts without an urban measure). Analysis using this variable is limited to urban tracts.

## Tests

The test suite (`tests/tests.py`) covers 25 assertions across 6 classes:

| Class | What it tests |
|---|---|
| `TestSchema` | All required columns present, correct dtypes |
| `TestMergeIntegrity` | Row count, no duplicate tracts, valid FIPS codes, both datasets contributed columns |
| `TestDataQuality` | Health outcomes in 0–100%, national means within CDC benchmarks, binary flags correct |
| `TestMissingData` | Zero nulls in key columns, expected null patterns in `lapop10`/`lapop1` |
| `TestAnalysisLogic` | Food desert tracts have higher diabetes/obesity, correlations in expected directions |
| `TestRegressionSmoke` | OLS fits without error, R² is valid, LILA coefficient is positive |
